# Pictographic

## Definition
A pictographic sign conveys meaning through a stylized picture of the thing it represents, rather than by spelling out sounds.

## Why it matters
Pictographs are the precursor category in the usual story of how writing developed: a picture of an ox meaning "ox" comes before any system that records the sounds of a word. As full writing systems, pictographs are limited, because not everything has a picture, which is why scripts moved toward representing sound. For design work the idea is alive and well: icons, wayfinding symbols, and signage are modern pictograms. Knowing the term, and how it differs from [ideographic](ideographic.md) and [logographic](logographic.md) signs, keeps designers precise about what an icon can and cannot carry on its own.

## Example
The figure on a restroom door or the bent-arrow exit sign is a pictogram: it shows a simplified picture and is read directly as meaning, with no spelling involved.

## Common mistake
Calling every non-alphabetic written symbol a "pictograph," or assuming a picture is universally understood. Pictographic legibility is cultural, not automatic: a symbol obvious to one audience can be opaque or misleading to another, which is why wayfinding systems test their icons rather than assuming them.

## In practice
- **An icon is a pictogram, so treat it like one:** lead with widely recognized conventions, pair an icon with a text label where comprehension matters, and test unfamiliar symbols with real users rather than trusting that the picture speaks for itself.
- **Keep the vocabulary straight:** a pictogram resembles its meaning; an [ideographic](ideographic.md) sign represents an idea more abstractly; a [logographic](logographic.md) character stands for a specific word or morpheme in a language. Using the right word avoids the common "icons are a universal language" overclaim.
