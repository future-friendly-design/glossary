# Tone mark

## Definition
A tone mark is a diacritic that shows the pitch or tone of a syllable in tonal languages.

## Why it matters
A tone mark is a specialized kind of [diacritic](diacritic.md), used where the pitch of a syllable changes a word's meaning, as in Mandarin Chinese and Vietnamese. In Hanyu Pinyin, the way Mandarin is written in the Latin alphabet, the four tones sit over a vowel as `ā`, `á`, `ǎ`, and `à`.

Vietnamese can stack a tone mark on top of another vowel mark, so one vowel may carry two marks at once. That stack rises higher than a single accent, so it needs more room between lines. How many marks pile on a letter is set by the language's [orthography](../language-terms/writing-systems-and-scripts/orthography.md), not the shared script, which is why the same Latin letters need more [line height](line-height.md) for Vietnamese than for most languages.

## Example
In Pinyin, `mā`, `má`, `mǎ`, and `mà` use tone marks to spell four different words.
